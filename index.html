Welcome to the Legacy of Chief James Nyanaro

This is more than a website — it's a living memory. A place to reflect, celebrate, and be inspired by a life of service, wisdom, and faith. Every section you explore is a piece of the story… of a man who led with humility and raised generations with grace.

Biography

🧒 Chapter One: Early Life

“From the humblest paths trodden barefoot to the firm ground of destiny, greatness often begins in the quiet belief that life holds more. Chief Nyanaro’s early journey teaches us that dreams shaped by struggle, faith, and perseverance can rise from even the most modest beginnings.”

Jeremiah 29:11 — “For I know the plans I have for you,” declares the Lord, “plans to prosper you and not to harm you, plans to give you a hope and a future.”

Have you ever dreamt of flying? Of reaching a goal so far beyond your current circumstances that it felt impossible? Chief James Nyanaro once did. And he believed. That belief would carry him far — beyond the fields of his childhood to the leadership of an entire region.

Born around 1936, in a time when calendars and clocks held little meaning in rural Kisii, his parents—Nyabaro Momanyi Nyaweri and Yusabia Kemunto, daughter of Mbee of the Bonyakoni clan—marked his arrival not by date, but by memory. His mother lovingly recalled that on the day he was born, a strange darkness fell across the land. It was the day the first motor vehicle, affectionately dubbed "Bota", passed through their village. That mysterious dimming of the sun? It was, as he later discovered, a solar eclipse — ‘Enyakoira’ in Ekegusii.

Years later, through historical records and personal reflection, he identified his birthday as 13th December 1936. A legend born in December, indeed.

Childhood was a time of wonder and quiet growth. With no shoes to wear and no modern clothes, the children wrapped themselves in shukas held with safety pins. Yet, it was a season of strength — where every task, every game, and every challenge taught resilience and shaped identity.

At age twelve, he underwent the traditional circumcision ceremony. Remarkably, another eclipse occurred on that very day in 1948. He took this as a divine sign — that God had marked his life for greatness. As the darkness gave way to light, so too did his path toward adulthood begin.

Determined to support his family and earn a living, young James ventured to Sotik Highlands to find work. Employed on European-owned farms, he started with a humble wage of five shillings and half a blanket per month. His relentless work ethic did not go unnoticed. Soon, he earned a raise and became a trusted errand boy, bringing food to tractor operators. It was here he forged his first lifelong friendship with Samwel Kabinga of Magwagwa.

After a short visit home, he resumed work — this time on a cattle ranch run by Magonya in a place called Set. The job involved clearing fields, and his pay rose to seven shillings. He worked alongside his agemate and friend Oeba Masanya. By the end of 1949, the two returned home for Christmas, their pockets lined with hard-earned savings.

With those savings, a new dream took root: education.

📘 Chapter Two: Education

“Education is the ladder that lifts one from obscurity to purpose. For Chief Nyanaro, each lesson learned was a step toward transformation — not just for himself, but for a community yet to dream. His journey teaches us that education is never just about books; it's about boldness, persistence, and the belief that knowledge can build nations.”

Proverbs 4:7 — “Wisdom is the principal thing; therefore get wisdom. And in all your getting, get understanding.”

In early 1950, I sat with my parents and elder brother Ndubi to discuss my dream of going to school. We joined others from our area in seeking formal education. Initially, we enrolled at Gekendo Mission School, a Catholic-founded institution. While my brother Ongaro chose Nyangoge DEB — a district school — we ultimately followed our personal callings, driven by belief: people go for what they want.

I began in Standard One under teacher Bernado Obiri, and in Standard Two, I was taught by Samwel Ondigi. Eventually, my savings from previous work ran out. To continue learning, I returned with friends to the Sotik Highlands to earn more money. Once we saved enough, we came back — only to find Gekendo had closed.

But a closed school didn’t mean a closed path. We transferred to Rangenyo School, enrolling in Standard Three as day scholars. Despite the distance and discomfort, I stayed committed. My earlier “eclipse days” felt like divine signs of purpose. I pushed on and reached Standard Four — but illness and registration delays meant I missed my exams. Still, Father Francis More allowed me to continue to Standard Five in 1955.

From there, my momentum built. I passed the CPE (Certificate of Primary Education), advanced to Standard Six, and eventually completed Standard Seven. In July 1959, I passed my Kenya African Primary Education exams.

Recognizing my performance, the priest at Rangenyo Mission offered me a job as an untrained teacher while I waited for a training opportunity. I worked diligently until December 1960, when I was finally accepted to Asumbi Teachers’ College. From February 1961 to November 1962, I trained and qualified as a P3 teacher.

In January 1963, I was employed and appointed as headteacher at Embonga Primary School. After two years, I transferred to Nyakenyomisia Primary in December 1964. There, I met Senior Chief Agustino Moturi, who inspired me to aim higher. I attempted my Form Two exams in 1965, but didn’t pass due to my workload. So, I stepped down as headteacher, transferred to Gekendo Primary, and focused on both teaching and studying.

With renewed dedication, I retook the Form Two exams in 1967 and passed — earning promotion to P2 teacher. It was a powerful reminder: those who leave their comfort zones can harvest greater rewards.

Emboldened, I registered for my Form Four exams through the London Education Council. It was no easy feat. I sat the exam four separate times — passing one paper at a time until I had all four. Persistence was my teacher. Giving up was never an option.

This relentless pursuit of learning wasn’t just about titles — it was about transforming my future, my family, and ultimately, my entire community.

🏫 Chapter Three: Working as a Teacher and Church Leader

“To teach is to touch lives forever. To lead in faith is to plant seeds of eternal hope. In classrooms and congregations, Chief Nyanaro sowed wisdom and watered purpose. His legacy reminds us that the greatest leaders are those who serve not just with their hands, but with their hearts.”

Matthew 5:14 — “You are the light of the world. A town built on a hill cannot be hidden.”

In 1971, after finally securing all four papers in the rigorous London Council exams, I qualified for promotion to P1 teacher. It was a moment of joy — earned through years of hard work, sacrifice, and persistence. When the education office offered me headteacher positions, I initially declined, preferring to remain close to students. Eventually, I accepted a deputy role and later took on the full responsibility as headteacher of Gekendo Primary School.

With passion and leadership, I spearheaded the growth of Gekendo Primary. As enrollment rose, the school expanded from two to four streams. The increased demand sparked a vision: to establish a secondary school where our students could continue learning. I led the school committee as secretary, and together we founded what would become a new Gekendo Secondary School.

Parents began transferring their children to our school due to the academic standards we upheld. With growing numbers, we established two branch schools — one at Sore and another at Nyanchoka. I was appointed as the headteacher of Nyanchoka branch. Within just a year, under my stewardship, the school flourished, earning me a new promotion from the education office.

In early 1972, I joined the Harambee committee tasked with building Kiabonyoru Secondary School. As treasurer of the board, I ensured transparency and accountability in handling the school’s funds. During that time, the education officer, Daniel Omangi, transferred me to Nyaramba Primary School, a posting that brought me closer to home.

While serving at Nyaramba, I was also appointed as the secretary of the parish council at Kebirigo Mission. I embraced the role, believing wholeheartedly that service to man is service to God. Balancing multiple leadership positions, I remained committed to both school and church duties — always guided by faith and a heart for community.

My presence at Nyaramba market became a daily ritual, where I shared tea and stories with locals. Among them were my uncle, Paul Ondieki, and his friend Wilson Gesaka. One day, they informed me that the community was rallying for me to succeed Chief Agustino Moturi Obwang’i, who was soon to retire.

I listened quietly, unsure whether it was genuine or political talk. I reminded them that I was simply a teacher, devoted to helping children chase their dreams and supporting the church’s mission. I had just been appointed chairperson of the parish committee to the Diocese of Kisii, serving under Bishop Mogendi. My heart was in teaching. My soul was in service.

✈️ Chapter Four: Travel to Rome

“Some journeys are more than just miles—they are spiritual awakenings. Chief Nyanaro’s pilgrimage to Rome was not only a journey across continents, but a deepening of faith, humility, and purpose. It showed that leadership guided by God is strengthened through sacred encounters and the courage to see beyond borders.”

Psalm 121:8 — “The Lord will watch over your coming and going both now and forevermore.”

Eventually, the news was confirmed — Chief Agustino Moturi had retired. Whispers from the community grew louder, calling for me to step into leadership. Many believed my years of grassroots development — from building schools and churches to guiding families — had prepared me for the mantle of chief. But as that conversation simmered, a new and sacred opportunity arose.

In 1975, the diocese organized a fully sponsored spiritual pilgrimage to Rome, Italy. During a parish council meeting, I was surprised and humbled to find my name among those selected. The church believed I was deserving — and perhaps, in their eyes and God’s, my journey of faith needed to expand beyond borders.

In May of that year, we departed aboard a Jumbo Jet, alongside believers from across Kenya — Nyeri, Malindi, Siaya, Namanga, and more. It was an enormous aircraft filled with faith-filled hearts. We first landed in France

From there, our journey continued by train, taking us two days through scenic routes toward the Mediterranean Sea. We arrived late in the evening, the same time we had set off — a poetic cycle of departure and arrival. Crossing the sea, we finally reached Vatican City in Rome.

The experience was unforgettable. We visited sacred sites, prayed fervently, and soaked in the spiritual atmosphere of the world's spiritual heart. It was not just sightseeing — it was a deep and holy moment of reflection and spiritual anchoring.

Upon returning home, we shared our testimonies at Kebirigo Mission and within our zone at Nyaramba. The pilgrimage had rekindled something powerful within me — not just faith, but a readiness to serve beyond myself, beyond the classroom, and even beyond the church.

💍 Chapter Five: Marriage

“A strong marriage is the heart of every lasting legacy. In choosing love, commitment, and faith, Chief Nyanaro and his beloved Mary built a family rooted in unity and service. Their bond was not just a personal blessing, but a foundation for generations — proving that love anchored in God endures all seasons.”

Ecclesiastes 4:9-10 — “Two are better than one, because they have a good return for their labor: If either of them falls down, one can help the other up.”

After our spiritual pilgrimage to Rome, my beloved wife Mary Bochaberi and I made the prayerful decision to solemnize our union in the church. Though we had already honored our cultural traditions through dowry, we desired to dedicate our marriage before God.

Our wedding took place at Nyaramba Catholic Church, officiated by Father Richard Queen. It was a moment of deep blessing and fulfillment — one that sealed our love in faith and spiritual unity.

Mary has been my unwavering companion through every chapter of life. Together, we were blessed with nine incredible children, each a gift and a light to our home:

  • Mercyline Moraa (born 1965)
  • Stella Mosinya (1967)
  • Kevin Bundi (1968)
  • Jane Kerubo (1972)
  • Rovert Kebaso (1974)
  • Pius Chelluget (1976)
  • Lillian Bosibori (1978)
  • Collette Kerubo (1980)
  • Geoffrey Samusi (1982)

They are not only my children but also my strength, my joy, and my legacy. Their support through every stage of my journey has been steadfast and humbling. As a family, we have held hands through both challenges and triumphs — always guided by faith and love.

To Mary, my rock and lifelong partner — I thank God for you. And to our children and their families, may the Lord bless you richly and guide you daily. I could not have asked for more.

🎖️ Chapter Six: Working as a Chief and Other Achievements

“Leadership is not given — it is earned through trust, vision, and selfless action. Chief Nyanaro rose not for titles, but for transformation. His service as chief was marked by compassion, wisdom, and a fierce dedication to his people. True greatness, as he showed, lies in building others, one decision, one school, one road, one soul at a time.”

Proverbs 11:14 — “Where there is no counsel, the people fall; but in the multitude of counselors there is safety.”

It was eventually confirmed — the position of Chief had officially been announced. What was once rumor became reality, and I felt called to serve. I submitted my application, and by God's grace, I was appointed and officially sworn in on 26th February 1976. My posting was at Ekerenyo, and I embraced the title “Chief” with great pride and responsibility.

The swearing-in was a grand occasion. Isaiah Chelluget, the Nyanza Provincial Commissioner, officiated the ceremony alongside the District Commissioners of Kisii and Nyamira — John Otika among them. Religious leaders prayed over me as the ceremonial chief’s hat was placed on my head. Father Richard Queen and Pastor Stephenson Maturi were among the spiritual leaders present. Also attending were our MP, Livingstone Ateba Mariita, and local counselor, Raphael Ondieki.

From the beginning, I committed myself to meaningful service. I helped spearhead the construction and expansion of schools, churches, roads, polytechnics, and health facilities. The people had placed their trust in me, and I worked diligently to fulfill their hopes.

I knew those earlier eclipses in my life were not just coincidence — they were signs of a divine path toward leadership. And through that divine guidance, I witnessed the birth of many developments: new schools, hospitals, and sustainable farming initiatives that ensured food security in every household.

My love for education remained strong. I chaired many school boards and contributed extensively to academic growth. Later, I was honored to serve as Chairperson of the Land Control Board in Ekerenyo, where we trained farmers on soil conservation and improved farming practices. Crops like maize, beans, coffee, and tea flourished, and livestock keeping improved family livelihoods.

My leadership journey continued as I rose to the position of Senior Chief, and eventually, Paramount Chief — overseeing all chiefs in the region. I underwent several training programs in Embu, Matuga, and Embaasi (Nairobi) to enhance my administrative and governance skills.

Together with fellow leaders, we founded the Gusii Cultural Council — a body of elders entrusted with guiding cultural, social, and political matters within the community. Our work shaped the identity and governance of our people for generations to come.

Among other projects, I helped establish:

  • New primary and secondary schools
  • Several youth polytechnics
  • The D.O.’s office in Ekerenyo and housing for police officers
  • Ekerenyo Health Centre, now a major hospital
  • Itibo Flower Society, supporting local flower farmers
  • Ekerubo Gietai Youth Institute and location offices for current administrators

To this day, I remain deeply proud of the work accomplished — and even more so, of my son Pius, who now serves in these very offices we once built. May the legacy of service, faith, and community never be forgotten.

🌅 Chapter Seven: Retirement

“Retirement is not the end of service — it is the beginning of reflection, mentorship, and legacy. Chief Nyanaro did not step away from duty; he stepped deeper into purpose, guiding others with wisdom hard-earned and freely shared. In rest, he still built. In quiet, he still led. True leaders never truly retire — they continue to inspire.”

2 Timothy 4:7 — “I have fought the good fight, I have finished the race, I have kept the faith.”

Though I eventually stepped down from my formal duties, the call to serve never truly faded. Retirement was not an end, but a gentle transition — from public office to quiet mentorship and continued contribution to community growth.

I remained involved in various projects, particularly in water access and school development. Wherever I was called upon, I offered my support willingly. It gave me joy to continue helping others, even without title or office.

The work I had done as Paramount Chief was recognized not only by the people but also by the government. At one point, I received an honorary recognition award from the President for exemplary service. Alongside the award came a generous monetary gift, which proved to be a blessing for my family.

That financial support enabled us to fund our youngest daughter’s journey to the United States for further education — a dream come true. It also allowed me to assist in the education of some of my grandchildren, whose parents were facing financial hardships. Thanks to that support, they were able to pursue their studies, fulfill their ambitions, and now sustain themselves and their families.

My greatest hope for them is that they, too, will carry forward the spirit of service. That they will reach out and support others in need — just as they once received help. In doing so, they will not only honor our family legacy but also bless countless others.

Even in retirement, I have remained guided by purpose — continuing to lead, love, and lift others, one act of kindness at a time.

🌟 Personal Messages to the Hero – Senior Chief James Nyanaro Nyabaro

📜 Message from Retired Senior Chief James Nyanaro to All

“To my beloved people, friends, leaders, church family, and generations to come…”

I stand today with a heart full of gratitude. I thank God, who has been my guide throughout my life. I thank my family, my community, my fellow leaders, and every soul who ever walked with me, supported me, and believed in me.

I served you with all my strength because I believed leadership is a calling to build others, not oneself. Together, we built schools, hospitals, churches, roads, and strong families. Together, we dreamed, we worked, and we grew.

I want to remind you, especially the young people, that greatness is born from humility, hard work, faith, and respect. Never give up on your dreams — I am living proof that a humble beginning does not determine the size of your destiny.

To my fellow leaders, lead with justice, honesty, and fear of God. To the church and elders, remain our pillars of wisdom. To my dear community, stay united in love and purpose.

I bless you all and thank you. May God bless North Mugirango and our beloved Kenya!

🏅 Achievements of Chief James Nyanaro

I. 🎓 Educational Milestones

  • Completed primary education at Rangenyo Mission School
  • Passed KAPE exams (1959)
  • Qualified as a P3 teacher at Asumbi Teachers College (1962)
  • Later upgraded to P2 and P1 after passing London Council exams
  • Served in multiple schools as teacher and headteacher, including Gekendo and Nyanchoka

II. 🏫 Contributions to Education Development

  • Expanded Gekendo Primary School from two to four streams
  • Helped found Sore and Nyanchoka branch schools
  • Instrumental in creating Kiabonyoru Secondary School and Ekerubo Gietai Youth Polytechnic
  • Mentored students and inspired a culture of education

III. 🏛 Administrative and Leadership Roles

  • Appointed Chief of Ekerenyo in 1976, then Senior and Paramount Chief
  • Supervised eight sublocations, mentored subchiefs
  • Co-founded the Gusii Cultural Council
  • Chaired the Land Control Board at Ekerenyo

IV. 🛠 Community Development Projects

  • Led construction of Ekerenyo Health Centre, roads, administrative offices, and police housing
  • Directed water access, soil preservation, and agricultural reform initiatives
  • Supported local flower growers through Itibo Flower Society

V. ⛪ Religious and Spiritual Service

  • Served as parish council secretary at Kebirigo Mission
  • Led church development committees and supported projects post-retirement
  • Participated in a spiritual pilgrimage to Rome and the Vatican in 1975

VI. 🏆 Honors and Recognition

  • Received official presidential recognition for outstanding service
  • Used monetary award to fund education for his children and grandchildren
  • Continued to be held in high esteem long after retirement

VII. 👨‍👩‍👧‍👦 Family Legacy

  • Father of nine children: Mercylyne Moraa, Stella Mosinya, Kevin Bundi, Jane, Kebaso, Pius Cheruget, Lillian, Collette, Geoffrey Samusi
  • Inspired leadership in the next generation — Pius now serves as assistant chief
  • Built a home grounded in faith, service, and unity

VIII. 🌱 Post-Retirement Service

  • Remained a respected elder and advisor
  • Continued to support school and church projects and agricultural programs
  • Served as a guiding example to youth and families

🌟 Extended Legacy Highlights

  • Mentor of Future Chiefs: Personally guided young administrators and church leaders who now serve across Kenya
  • Cultural Stewardship: Balanced traditional Kisii wisdom with modern governance
  • Voice of Stability: Promoted peace during politically unstable times through public talks and quiet diplomacy
  • Elder Statesman: Hosted national figures and regional clergy, offering counsel beyond his official duties
  • Moral Influence: Championed clean living, rejecting alcohol, corruption, and moral decline

Chief Nyanaro’s impact was not confined to his time in office — it lives on through his family, the institutions he helped build, and the countless lives he touched with wisdom, kindness, and unwavering faith.
